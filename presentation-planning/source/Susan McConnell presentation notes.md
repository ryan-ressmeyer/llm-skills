
### 1. Slide Design & Formatting

Your slides should act as a visual aid, not a teleprompter or a cluttered dashboard.

- **Typography:** Use Sans Serif fonts (e.g., Arial, Helvetica) because they are much easier for an audience to read quickly on a projector. Keep font sizes between 18 and 36 point (use 14 point strictly for citations). Never use ALL CAPS.
    
- **Color & Contrast:** Stick to high-contrast combinations. A plain white background with dark text is best for well-lit rooms to keep people awake. However, **a black background with white text is ideal for viewing fluorescence, photomicrographs, or detailed visual stimuli**, as it allows the audience's eyes to dark-adapt.
    
- **Avoid the Red/Green Trap:** Never use red and green together to differentiate data points. It is unreadable for colorblind audience members, and red psychophysically reads as an "angry" color.
    
- **The "Statement" Heading:** Every slide must have a heading, and that heading should be a simple declarative sentence summarizing the slide's bottom-line conclusion (e.g., "Protein X represses Gene Y"), rather than a static title (e.g., "Results of Protein X").
    
- **Whitespace:** Leave generous empty space on the sides and bottom of your slides. Projectors often misalign and cut off edges, and people in the back row won't be able to see text sitting at the very bottom of the screen.
    

### 2. Managing Text and Images

If the audience has to choose between reading a wall of text and listening to you, they will stop listening.

- **The 2-Line Rule:** Never use more than two lines of text in a single block. If you have a long quote, paraphrase it rather than forcing the audience to read it.
    
- **An Image on Every Slide:** Aim to include at least one simple image or diagram on every single slide. This accommodates different learning styles simultaneously: visual (the image), auditory (your voice), and reading (the statement heading).
    
- **Pacing:** The old "one slide per minute" rule is dead. If you keep your slides simple and focus on one specific point per slide, you can move through them much faster.
    
- **Animations:** Use simple animations (like "Appear") to unveil bullet points or complex data progressively. Only show the audience what you are actively talking about. Avoid distracting, flashy slide transitions (like spinning cubes); stick to a simple cut, or a "wipe" if it conceptually matches the science.
    

### 3. The "Minimum Essential Data" Rule

Think of your data like minimum essential medium for cell cultures: provide exactly what is needed for the claim to survive, and nothing more.

- **Strip the Extraneous:** Never throw up a massive, complex figure from a published paper and say, _"I know this is busy, but just look at this one part."_ Export the figure to Photoshop or an editor, crop out the irrelevant panels, erase acronyms you won't define, and recolor it to match your presentation.
    
- **The Golden Rule:** If you are not going to explicitly explain an axis, a line, or a label to the audience, delete it from the slide.
    

### 4. Talk Structure & The "Home Slide"

Human attention spans inherently crash in the middle of a talk. You have to build in rescue points.

- **The Dive and Regroup:** Structure your talk as a series of "data dives." Start broad, dive deep into the technical weeds of a specific experiment, and then come back up for air to summarize before diving into the next experiment.
    
- **The Home Slide:** Create a simple, recurring summary graphic (like a cartoon of your overarching neural pathway or system architecture). Show this exact same slide every time you transition between data dives. It acts as a visual anchor, allowing audience members who zoned out to catch up on where you are and what you are doing next.
    
- **The Conclusion:** Never use "false endings" (e.g., saying "To conclude..." when you are only finishing part one). When you actually reach the end, zoom back out to answer the massive, big-picture questions you posed in your introduction.
    

### 5. The Q&A Hack

- **Don't End on Acknowledgments:** Acknowledge your collaborators and funders, but when you finish speaking and open the floor for Q&A, click forward to leave your **Summary/Conclusion slide** on the screen. It helps the audience formulate intelligent questions, and it gives you a visual reference to point to while answering them.
