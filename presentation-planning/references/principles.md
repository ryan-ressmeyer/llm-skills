# Scientific Presentation Principles

Seven principles that recur across the source literature (Naegle 2021; McConnell, Stanford lecture; Alley, *Craft of Scientific Presentations*; Alon, *Molecular Cell*, "How To Give a Good Talk"; Tufte, *Visual Display*; Doumont, *Trees, Maps, Theorems*; Crivellaro / Olson, *Winning Formula* / ABT; Kenny, *Public Speaking for Scientists and Engineers*).

Each principle is stated, then translated into a concrete test you can apply to a draft.

---

## 1. The talk is a spoken performance; the slides are visual aids

**Principle.** The audience listens to the speaker and glances at the slide. They cannot read and listen at the same time — attending to one means ignoring the other (Doumont). The slide exists to do what the voice cannot: show the shape of the data, anchor a claim visually, let an audience member who zoned out recover.

**Test.** If a slide, read silently without the speaker, teaches as much as the talk, the slide is doing the speaker's job. Strip words from the slide until the speaker is necessary.

---

## 2. One core message, stated first, restated last

**Principle.** A talk is not a list of findings; it is an argument for a single sentence. That sentence is the ABT (AND — context / BUT — conflict / THEREFORE — resolution; Olson via Crivellaro), the "package of information you wish to retain" (Kenny Ch. 6), the "big-picture answer" (McConnell). Everything on every slide is scaffolding for it.

**Test.** Can the speaker state the one sentence they want every audience member to remember? If not, the talk is not ready. State the sentence at minute one and again at the close.

---

## 3. Assertion–Evidence, not Topic–Bullets

**Principle.** Alley's rule, reinforced by McConnell's "statement heading": the slide title is a complete declarative sentence — the conclusion of the slide. The body is the single piece of visual evidence that supports the title. The PowerPoint default (topic phrase + bulleted list) is the failure mode.

**Test.** Every slide title should read like a sentence of the abstract. "Results of Protein X" fails. "Protein X represses Gene Y" passes.

---

## 4. Narrative order beats chronology

**Principle.** The listener has a file-card memory, not a book index (Kenny Ch. 11). A novel holds attention better than a textbook because it uses flashback, re-entry, and deferred reveal. "We did X, then Y failed, so we did Z" is the #1 failure mode in scientific talks (Alon, McConnell).

**Test.** Does the talk start with a question the audience cares about (hook), present a conflict (why the question is hard), and resolve it? Or does it start with "Background" and end with "Future Directions"? The former is a talk. The latter is a lab notebook read aloud.

---

## 5. Every concept is earned before it is used

**Principle.** A prerequisite dumped as "Background" before Results is wasted — the audience doesn't yet know why they should care. A prerequisite woven into the narrative as a stepping stone (introduced at the moment it's needed, always as forward motion) is retained.

**Test.** Walk the deck in order. For every unfamiliar term or concept a slide uses, find the earlier slide where it was introduced. If the introduction was a stand-alone "Background" slide, rework it into a narrative beat.

---

## 6. Minimum essential data, with progressive reveal

**Principle.** Tufte's data-ink ratio — every pixel should carry information. McConnell's "if you won't explain the axis, delete the axis." Never paste a published figure and say "look at this part." The published figure was optimized for a reader with infinite time; the talk figure must be optimized for a glance. Complex figures should be built up piece by piece, revealing only the part currently being discussed.

**Test.** For every element on every slide — axis, label, curve, legend entry, panel — can the speaker name a sentence they will say about it? If not, delete the element or defer it with a build.

---

## 7. Attention crashes mid-talk; build in recovery

**Principle.** The audience cannot sustain full attention for 30 minutes. Periodic "zoom out to the big-picture question" beats give zoned-out audience members a way back in (McConnell's "dive and regroup," Kenny's "interest revival").

**Devices — optional, not required:**
- A recurring schematic (the "home slide") that reappears between data dives. *Useful for long talks with clearly separable sections.*
- Verbal signposting: "So we've seen that X. But the question was Y. Let me show you how X speaks to Y."
- A progressive-build overview that fills in sections as the talk progresses.

**Test.** At minute 15 of a 30-minute talk, does a hypothetical zoned-out listener have a way to re-enter? If the answer requires reading the slide, it is not recovery — it is homework.

## Numerical rules of thumb

Not principles, but operational defaults from the source literature:

| Rule | Source | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 100 words per minute spoken | Kenny Ch. 6 | Slower than conversation. Slower still at the opening and closing. |
| 18–36 pt font, sans-serif | Kenny Ch. 7; Naegle | 14 pt acceptable for citations only. |
| ≤ 2 lines of text per block | McConnell | If longer, paraphrase. |
| No red–green pairings | McConnell; accessibility convention | ~8% of men are red-green colorblind. |
| Dark background for fluorescence/microscopy; light background for diagrams/text | McConnell | Let the audience's eyes adapt to the kind of image. |
| Viewing-distance : lettering-height ≈ 300 : 1 | Kenny Ch. 7 | At 10 m, letters should be ~3 cm tall in projection. |
| Pause before opening; pause before closing | Kenny Ch. 6; Crivellaro | Pause is the cheapest attention device. |
| Do not end on acknowledgments; leave summary slide up during Q&A | McConnell | The screen during Q&A is your visual reference. |
| Do not say "thank you" or apologize at end | Kenny Ch. 6 | Descending pitch, pause, sit down. |
