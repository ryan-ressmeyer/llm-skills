INTRODUCTION

Your scientific presentations are a critical part
of your career, and it is crucial to deliver them
as effectively as possible. But there are many
obstacles along the road to successful
presentation delivery. Therefore, make your
professional life easier and more prosperous
by crafting your next presentation using the
Effective Presentation Winning Formula
presented in this book.

This book is short and intended for
everyone who needs to design and deliver a
10-15-minute scientific presentation. The
book is meant for bachelor and master
students, PhD students, postdocs, researchers,
assistant and full professors, especially those
ALAN CRIVELLARO

who want to communicate better.

The first part of this book describes the
importance of planning a compelling
narrative for your presentation. The middle
part of this book explains the Effective
Presentation Winning Formula, a 10-step
process to plan and design your presentation.
The third and final part briefly discusses the
process of preparing supporting slides and
presentation delivery.

What is an effective presentation?

At scientific conferences, the audience listens
to many presentations in a day, and it is
exhausting. But despite presenters’ efforts,
conference participants will soon forget most
of the presentations.

Only an effective presentation leaves a
long-lasting impression on the audience.
Moreover, an effective presentation clearly
demonstrates the connection the presenter
has to the topic and stimulates discussion.

EFFECTIVE SCIENTIFIC PRESENTATIONS

Why do we present at a meeting ora
conference? Because we want to engage

with other scientists.

As a presenter, you must work hard to
make your message stand out from the many
other presentations. To reach this aim, you
need to start thinking about the effectiveness
of your presentation.

Before we go any further, let me just say
that the Effective Presentation Winning
Formula is not an attempt to come up with a
rigid structure of a presentation. Rather, it is
merely a tool that will help presenting
scientists to design effective presentations.

How to design an effective presentation?

Presenting your ideas, either at a group
meeting or at a big conference, is part of your
academic life. Presenting contributes to
defining your role in the scientific community
to which you belong. |

But effective scientific presentations are

hard to design and deliver, unless you start
early by thinking about your goal and, most
importantly, by crafting a clear message for
your audience.

What is the one thing you want people to
remember from your presentation?

This guide will show you how to create a 10-
15-minute effective scientific presentation to
deliver your message. You need to design your
presentation so it will deliver a clear takeaway to
your audience, which will leave a long-lasting
impression and ultimately boost your career.

The key to success

The first stage of planning an effective
presentation is to define three key elements:

e Target audience: does your presentation
target all the conference participants, a
specific group within them, or a single
person?

e Goal: why are you going to deliver
your presentation? What do you want
to achieve?

e Message: what is the one thing you
want your audience to remember
about your presentation?

The best way to deliver an effective

presentation is to plan it.

Answer these questions specifically referring
to your next presentation. Then follow the
Effective Presentation Winning Formula to
design the content of your presentation
according to a clear path. It will help your
audience grasp the critical information early on
and navigate through the presentation smoothly.

The next pages will introduce you each
step of the Effective Presentation Winning
Formula. Then an example will demonstrate
how the Effective Presentation Winning
Formula can be applied. Lastly, a diagram will
provide you with all you need to have at hand
to craft your next presentation.

FORMULA

1. Craft your core statement

Begin preparing your presentation by
clarifying your message using the power of
storytelling with the ABT (i.e. And, But,
Therefore) narrative framework formalized
by Dr Randy Olson. The core statement of
your presentation will only be one to three
sentences in length. Follow the template to
craft it.

At first, start your ABT with a simple
opening description that focuses on what the

7

ALAN CRIVELLARO

presentation is about. Second, define precisely
why the audience should care about it. Then
state what the problem is and how it became
so. Lastly, provide your solution and explain
how it changes how we see the topic now.

ABT template:

[what] and [why it is important] but
[problem/question] therefore

[solution/answer].

Here is an example: “The eggs of birds vary
in form, AND we think this is driven by
evolution, BUT we lack a complete overview
to demonstrate it. THEREFORE, I studied
thousands of eggs and found that bird flight
capability is the drive of egg shape variation.”

You will start your presentation with the
ABT sentence you just crafted. Creating
contrast and instability with the “but”
statement will excite listeners’ brains and
create the right conditions to get them
engaged and remember your presentation.

EFFECTIVE SCIENTIFIC PRESENTATIONS

2. Define the impact of your topic

After listening to your ABT introduction
sentence, your target audience is straightway
engaged with you and your presentation.

Now it is time to provide more evidence on
the importance of investigating the topic you
are studying. It is not sufficient to say,
“Studying fish stock in the Northern Sea is
important.” You need to explain why this is
important. Provide evidence that links your
case with the research your target audience is
currently conducting. Show that you know
and understand what they are doing and how
your work is related to their work.

Why should the audience listen to your
presentation?

Give them 3 good reasons!

ALAN CRIVELLARO

3. Explain why you are an expert

In this step, it will be beneficial if, alongside
your name, you introduce yourself adding
any pertinent information to demonstrate
your credibility concerning the topic. Maybe
you are completing your PhD on the subject,
so you share that accomplishment. If you
have already published a few papers on the
topic, then mention the specific details, so the
audience will connect you to the articles.
This step marks the end of the introduction
of your presentation. If you did it effectively,
the audience is now willing to listen to you to
know more about the topic. Therefore, it is
time to provide further substantial content.

4. Provide details on the topic

Share the details on the actual context around
the topic of your presentation. With your
initial ABT, you only provided a glimpse into
it; now the audience is looking for compelling
information. Refer to previous work of other
scientists and remember that those scientists

10

EFFECTIVE SCIENTIFIC PRESENTATIONS

might be in the audience. So, it is terribly
important to acknowledge where our current
understanding is, how we got there and what
has been done so far. It does not mean you
need to provide an historical perspective on
the topic, but quote the fundamental points
needed to fully appreciate your contribution
in the field.

Refer to the “ and ” part

of your ABT and expand it.

Keep in mind your final message, as you
want to provide the facts and supporting
information so audience members can
understand your message. But avoid
overloading the audience with information.
Providing too many details will cause your
presentation to become like white noise that
will soon put your audience off. Your aim
here is to maximize the signal (ie. your
message) by supporting it with current
knowledge, but reduce the noise avoiding any
unnecessary information.

ALAN CRIVELLARO

5. State the problem you are addressing

After an introduction on the topic, the
audience got all the information needed to
understand it. It is now time to create contrast
by introducing the problem in a way that
attracts attention. Do not bumble. The
problem should sound like a clear threat for
which we urgently need to find a solution. If
you prefer, you can state the problem as a
question.

What is “the one” problem

you are trying to solve?

“The one” question you want to

answer during your presentation?

12

EFFECTIVE SCIENTIFIC PRESENTATIONS

6. Describe the materials and methods

From now on, you will guide your audience
on the journey to find the solution to the
problem. There are some necessary stops you
must take along the way, where you will
describe the materials and methods you
applied to conduct your study. Provide only
the details required to understand the
solution to the problem. You can always
provide the specifics about the materials and
methods later on in the question and answer
session.

How did you discover the solution
to the problem?

How did you find out the answer
to the question?

13

ALAN CRIVELLARO

7. Present only the main result(s)

After describing the materials and methods
you used, reveal the results and do not keep
the audience waiting too long for the reveal.
Results are most likely what your audience is
waiting for, and they want to know what you
achieved.

Communicate your main result with a
straightforward key sentence. Think about it as
an advertising slogan. Then explain all the
details required to support it. If you have more
than one result, describe it with the same
pattern: a key sentence, followed by additional
information.

If you show graphs, diagrams, photos,
drawings, schematics and maps make sure all
are simple to understand and describe them in
detail. Never show a figure you are not going
to describe.

14
EFFECTIVE SCIENTIFIC PRESENTATIONS

8. Explain how your results are useful

How do you see the world now that we

have your new results?

Refer to point 2, “Define the impact of your
topic,” on the Effective Presentation Winning
Formula where you stated why the audience
should listen to your presentation and
provide evidence that your presentation was
worth listening to.

In this step, you are showing how you are
bringing the topic forward by means of your
results.

9. Provide a solution to the problem

Eventually it is time to provide a final, clear and
concise solution to the problem. This needs to be
a key moment in your presentation. What you
are aiming for is a clear message which
summarizes your presentation in a few simple
words. This is what you want your presentation
to be remembered for.

ALAN CRIVELLARO

Think about the following scene. At the
conference venue you are walking along an
aisle and two other participants are walking
towards you. They look at you and smile. As
soon as they pass by, you catch one of the
whisperings: “s/he is the one who talked
about...”. How would you like this person to
continue the sentence? The answer to this
question is what you need to clearly state at
this point of your presentation.

10. Recap

Now it is time to repeat your message again,
using your initial ABT core message.
Restating your key message ensures that the
audience will hear it again and can relate it to
what you just discussed during the
presentation. This is a good time to mention
the details if you have plans to continue the
research on the topic. Now your audience will
know that you will keep working on this and
have more results to share.

The following text represents the script of a
hypothetical 10-minute scientific presentation.
I am not suggesting you write your
presentation (but feel free to do so if you like the
idea) and then read it while on stage, I rather
aim at providing a practical illustration on how
the Effective Presentation Winning Formula is
applied in a concrete example. The numbers
between square brackets represent each of the
10 steps described by the Effective Presentation
Winning Formula.

Title: Avian egg shape: Form, function, and
evolution.

[1 - Core statement] The eggs of birds
exhibit a variety of forms, and their shape is
generally explained as an adaptation to life

history. But we currently lack a global
| synthesis of how egg shape differences arise

and evolve. Therefore, we studied egg shape
diversity in birds and found that flight
capability is a driver of egg shape variation.

[2 - Impact] Avian eggs provide a simple but
powerful system in which to explore the
physical and evolutionary drivers. of
morphological diversity. Our thinking
suggests that a bird adapted for high-powered
flight may maximize egg size by increasing
egg asymmetry and/or ellipticity, while
maintaining a streamlined body plan. Moving
forward, it will be important to determine
how the developmental process of egg
shaping is coupled, in terms of physiology and
genetics, with evolutionary constraints
associated with flight strength and efficiency.

[3 - Expert] I have been working on this
topic over the past three years during my
PhD. In this presentation I am going to share
my most recent analyses on the dataset that
my co-authors and I published last year in
Scientific Data.

[4 - Details] The eggs of birds exhibit a
variety of sizes and shapes. A hypothesis for
the adaptive function of egg shape typically

birds laying conical eggs that roll in a tight
circle; an increase in incubation efficiency
when egg shape is associated with the
number of eggs in a clutch; or other
advantages related to strength, diet, and
development.

For example, spherical eggs might be
advantageous because the sphere is
uniformly strong and would be robust to
incidental damage in the nest. In contrast,
conical eggs may be beneficial because they
can accommodate an increased concentration
of pores at the blunt end, creating a
specialized respiratory site for accelerated

Previous studies investigating the diversity
of egg shape have been hampered by small

comprehensive data on egg morphometry
and life history traits, and therefore most of

[5 - Problem] So, most of the above

[6 - Materials and Methods] To resolve this
issue, we quantified egg shape using a
morphometric analysis of 49,175 eggs
representing ~1400 (~14% total) species in 35
extant orders, plus two extinct orders. We
then use these data to (i) quantify the
observed morphologies in a two-dimensional
morphospace for egg shape and (ii) test major
hypotheses about egg shape variation in the
context of key life history and environmental

[7 - Results] Our investigation of

First, egg shape is a continuum, with no

Second, avian clades differ widely in their

DESIGN VISUALS

Design your slides and figures

Now that you have defined the narrative of
your presentation, it is time to think about the
slides you need to show to support it. What is
the best way to visually support your
presentations? Each slide can represent one
or more points of the Effective Presentation
Winning Formula and should only convey
one piece of information or idea.

Follow the rule of one idea per slide,
one slide per idea.

25
ALAN CRIVELLARO

Strong parsimony should guide your slide
design. Use a simple slide design with limited
text and one or only a few figures per slide. As
for your speech, keep the message of each slide
very clear. Do not blur it with too much
graphics, animations or excess of text. The
slides should support your talk, not distract
your audience with text, plots, and animations.

The “slides sorted” function allows you to
have a general overlook at all the slides. Use
this view mode to have a general overview of
your presentation and make sure you can
identify the main points of your speech while
looking at all the slides. If each slide conveys
its message when it is very small on your
screen, then it will be clear also on a big screen
in presentation mode.

Just before your presentation

Your narrative and your slides are now ready.
What is next? Well, it is time to get on stage!
Right from the first moment you must
engage with your audience. Your audience
should feel part of an engaging conversation.
So keep eye contact with your public, speak
toward people (not facing the screen or your
computer) and keep an open body posture.
You are the one who conducted the study,
and you know everything about it. Let the
audience be part of it too. Bring the audience

into your study by guiding them step by step

27

ALAN CRIVELLARO

toward the look for a solution to the problem
you stated. An effective way to engage with
your audience is to share the emotions you
felt while conducting specific steps of your
study. You would never write your emotions
or feelings in a scientific paper, but there is
nothing wrong about sharing them in a
presentation.

And if your mouth gets dry, think about
biting a slide of lemon. It helps!

Start and lead your presentation

Before you deliver your presentation, you
need to get your audience connected to you.
How? With two powerful tools: a pause and
your ABT core statement. A brief pause, 1-2
seconds of silence, will raise the audience
members’ curiosity and expectations. All
attendees will get in the mind-set to listen to
your first sentences and grasp your message.
Therefore, right after a pause is the perfect
moment to speak out loud and clear your ABT.
And then pause again to let your audience
assimilate the message. The ABT ensures that

28

EFFECTIVE SCIENTIFIC PRESENTATIONS

your message will leave an impression on the
audience members.

Deliver your presentation with a calm
voice; but remember that it is important to
change your tone to emphasize essential
steps. Pause before delivering critical
information. Give the audience time to
receive and process your message.

Right after your presentation: Q&A

You are now through with your presentation,
but you are not done yet! An effective
presentation will leave the audience
interested in the topic, and they might have
questions, doubts, or curiosities that you need
to answer and satisfy.

Probably the most challenging part in the
Q&A is to listen and understand the
questions. Focus on the person asking you a
question and listen up to the last word he/she
says. Only then can you start answering the
questions. Most likely, you know how to
answer them, as you are the expert on the
' topic you presented. However, it might be

29

ALAN CRIVELLARO

worth thinking in advance what to say if you
have no idea how to answer.

Many questions can be easily predicted, so
feel free to prepare a slide and support your
answer to those easy-to-predict questions.
